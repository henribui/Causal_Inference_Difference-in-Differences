# Labor Migration and Human Capital Formation

Replication of Dinkelman & Mariotti (2016), which examines the long-run effects of circular 
labor migration on educational attainment in communities of origin in Malawi. The paper 
exploits exogenous variation in emigration conditions — specifically the lifting of restrictions 
on Wenela recruitment in 1967 and the subsequent ban in 1974 — to identify causal effects 
across different cohorts and districts.

The analysis covers three main components. First, descriptive evidence on educational 
attainment across treatment cohorts defined by exposure to migration shocks. Second, 
validity checks through a difference-in-differences framework comparing pretreatment and 
late treatment cohorts across districts with and without Wenela stations. Third, DiD 
estimation of the causal effect of migration on average years of schooling and share of 
adults with any primary schooling, including a condensed model with cohort, gender, and 
district fixed effects and baseline covariate trend interactions.

All analysis conducted in R using R Markdown. Methods: Difference-in-differences, fixed 
effects, balance checks, subgroup analysis.

**Paper:** Dinkelman, T. & Mariotti, M. (2016). *The Long-Run Effects of Labor Migration 
on Human Capital Formation in Communities of Origin.* AEJ: Applied Economics, 8(4): 1-35.
